### Background

The biopsychosocial (BPS) model, introduced by Engel (1977), marked a paradigm shift from purely biomedical approaches by proposing that biological, psychological, and social factors interact in the development and maintenance of health conditions. In chronic pain research, the BPS model has become the dominant framework, endorsed by major pain organizations and integrated into clinical guidelines.

Despite its widespread adoption, systematic analysis of how the BPS model is operationalized in scientific literature is lacking. Previous work (e.g., Crombez, 2015; https://osf.io/preprints/osf/ug2mr_v1) suggests that the BPS model often functions as a meta-theoretical perspective rather than a specific explanatory framework, serving roles such as an organizing principle, a justification for interdisciplinary approaches, or a rhetorical device.

This review addresses that gap by examining how reviews claiming a BPS framework actually use it. Specifically, it will document patterns of BPS usage, assess the scope, balance, and integration of biological, psychological, and social factors, and map psychological concepts and their theoretical underpinnings. By doing so, the review will clarify whether the BPS model is applied as an integrative framework or merely as a label, and will provide a foundation for improving conceptual clarity in pain research.

### Primary research question(s)

1. How is the BPS model operationalized in reviews of chronic pain?
2. What is the scope, balance, and integration of biological, psychological, and social factors in musculoskeletal pain reviews?
3. Which psychological concepts and theoretical frameworks are used in chronic pain literature?

### Secondary research question(s)

1. What conceptual problems (e.g., vague definitions, …) are identified in reviews using the BPS model?

### Expectations / hypotheses

The BPS model will be operationalized inconsistently and predominantly as a rhetorical device across reviews. Specifically:

- Many reviews will use the BPS model as a rhetorical or organizing device rather than as an integrative explanatory framework.
- True mechanistic integration of biological, psychological, and social factors will be rare; most reviews will present domains in parallel (multifactorial rather than interactive).
- Psychological factors will dominate coverage, while biological and social domains will often be minimal or absent.
- Integration statements, when present, will typically lack detail on pathways or mechanisms.
- Conceptual clarity issues (e.g., vague definitions, overlapping constructs) will be common, especially for psychological concepts.

These expectations are informed by prior conceptual critiques (e.g., Crombez, 2015) and the observed tendency for reviews to emphasize psychological factors in chronic pain research.

### Dependent variable(s) / outcome(s) / main variables

Main variables of interest:

- BPS framework usage patterns (e.g., location in record, function such as justification, organizing principle, conclusion).
- Coverage of domains: Biological, psychological, and social factors (extent: elaborated, mentioned, minimal, absent).
- Integration quality: Whether interactions between domains are specified (mechanistic, directional, descriptive, none).
- Psychological concepts: Names, definitions, hierarchical & semantic relationships, theoretical frameworks (e.g., fear-avoidance, illness perception).
- Balance assessment: Relative emphasis on biological vs. psychological vs. social domains.
- Typology of BPS operationalization: True integrative, multifactorial, pseudo-BPS, rhetorical BPS.

### Independent variable(s) / intervention(s) / treatment(s)

This review does not concern interventions or treatments. Instead, it examines conceptual explanatory factors as reported in included reviews. No comparator or control groups apply, as this is a conceptual synthesis of review literature.

## Screening

In this section, you register your screening procedure: the procedure you designed to eliminate all irrelevant sources from the results of the search strategy (and retain the relevant sources).

### Screening stages

The screening process will occur in three stages:

1. **Stage 1: Title and Abstract Screening**
   - Purpose: Eliminate records that do not meet inclusion criteria (e.g., no mention of “biopsychosocial” in title/abstract, not a review, wrong population, …).
   - Method: Manual screening by a human reviewer using Rayyan.
   - Deduplication: Performed prior to screening using Rayyan’s automated deduplication feature.
   - Masking: None (titles, abstracts, and authors visible).

2. **Stage 2: Abstract-Level coding and classification by Pain Type**
   - Purpose: Categorize included reviews by ICD-11 pain categories.
   - Method: Manual classification based on abstract information.
   - Output: Reviews coded as “musculoskeletal” or “unspecified” will proceed to Stage 3.
   - AI Use: Eligibility decisions will be based solely on human screening; GenAI may assist with coding classification in Stage 2, subject to human review.

3. **Stage 3: Full-Text Screening**
   - Purpose: Confirm eligibility for musculoskeletal pain reviews and prepare for detailed coding.
   - Method: Manual screening by human reviewers.
   - AI Use: Eligibility decisions will be based solely on human screening; GenAI may assist with coding classification in Stage 3, subject to human review.

### Deduplication

- All search results will be exported in .ris format and imported into Rayyan.
- Automated deduplication will be applied before Stage 1 screening.

The synthesis will be conducted by one primary synthesist (the lead reviewer) to ensure conceptual coherence across quantitative and qualitative components. No independent synthesis procedures (e.g., dual synthesis) are planned due to the interpretive nature of the review and resource constraints. However, reliability will be supported indirectly through prior steps:

- Double coding during extraction for subsets (20% of records) to ensure consistency in the underlying data.
- Consensus discussions and PI adjudication for any discrepancies in coding that feed into synthesis.

All synthesis decisions, including typology assignments and conceptual interpretations, will be documented in the OSF audit trail for transparency.

### Synthesis reconciliation procedure

Because the synthesis will be conducted primarily by one synthesist, formal reconciliation procedures for divergent synthesis decisions are not planned. However, if additional team members contribute to interpretation (e.g., during typology assignment or thematic synthesis), any differences in conceptual interpretation will be addressed through structured consensus discussions. These discussions will follow a documented decision framework based on the predefined inference criteria in the codebook. If consensus cannot be reached, the Principal Investigator will adjudicate and provide the final decision. All reconciliation steps and rationales will be logged in the OSF audit trail to ensure transparency and reproducibility.

### Synthesis procedure justification

The synthesis procedures were designed to balance scientific rigor with practical feasibility, given the conceptual nature of this review. Planned data transformations—such as recoding free-text objectives into predefined categories and mapping psychological concepts to established taxonomies—are justified by their necessity for comparability and interpretability across heterogeneous sources. These transformations rely on assumptions of conceptual stability within pain research frameworks, which are supported by prior literature and pilot coding exercises. Data integrity will be safeguarded through systematic cross-checks against source texts, reliability metrics on double-coded subsets, and validation of AI-assisted classifications by human reviewers. Missing or invalid data will be flagged and either corrected or excluded, with all decisions documented in the OSF audit trail. The synthesis plan, combining quantitative descriptive summaries and qualitative framework synthesis, was chosen to reflect the dual goals of mapping patterns and interpreting conceptual depth. Inference criteria—such as predefined rules for integration quality and typology assignment—ensure consistency and transparency in conclusions. No blinding is applied because conceptual interpretation requires contextual awareness (e.g., journal, authorship), and risk of bias is minimal given the non-interventional nature of the review. Reliability assurance is embedded in extraction stages rather than synthesis itself, as only one synthesist will conduct the final synthesis; however, reconciliation procedures are in place should additional contributors be involved. These design choices collectively optimize methodological rigor while respecting resource constraints.

### Description

This review investigates how the biopsychosocial (BPS) model is actually applied in pain research. It will analyze reviews claiming a BPS framework to identify usage patterns, evaluate the scope and integration of biological, psychological, and social factors, and map psychological concepts and their theoretical bases. The goal is to clarify whether the BPS model functions as an integrative framework or merely as a label, providing a foundation for greater conceptual clarity in chronic pain research.
